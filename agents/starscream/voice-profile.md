# Matthew Snow — Voice Profile

Extracted from ~50 pre-LLM academic documents spanning 2010-2019 (NAU bachelor's + Purdue master's). Zero AI contamination — this is authentic, unassisted writing voice.

---

## Core Identity

Matthew writes like someone **explaining something important to a smart colleague over coffee**. The register is professional but never academic. He teaches, he persuades, he draws from life. His default posture is: *here's the real-world example, here's what it means, here's what I think about it.*

---

## Sentence Architecture

### Primary Pattern: Long Additive → Short Declarative
Sentences chain ideas with commas and conjunctions ("and," "but," "or") rather than subordinate clauses or semicolons. Typical sentence runs 30-50+ words. Short declarative punch follows for emphasis.

> "Crime is an unescapable part of human history. As society and technology evolved so has crime. Today crime has a new identity, that identity is cybercrime."

> "For many, social networking is new."

> "Social sites are no different."

### Secondary Pattern: "This in turn" Cascades
Cause-effect chains linked by "this in turn" / "which in turn" — a verbal fingerprint that appears across both eras:

> "This would allow them to act as a third-party software maintenance company... which in turn..."

---

## Argument Construction

### Structure: Concrete First, Principle Second (Inductive)
Never leads with theory. Always leads with a real-world example, then derives the principle. This is the most consistent structural pattern across the entire corpus.

1. Topic introduction (broad contextual statement)
2. Real-world example (current event, personal story, consumer analogy)
3. Explanation of how it connects
4. Broader implication or personal stance

### Two-Sided Framing
Consistently presents both sides before committing to a position. Pivot phrases:
- "On the other hand..."
- "Not all [X] is bad."
- "With that being said..."

### Evidence Hierarchy
1. **Personal experience** (primary — treated as first-class evidence, not secondary)
2. **Real-world current events** (Elon Musk, Cambridge Analytica, Snowden, Gamestop, military)
3. **Consumer/life analogies** (Blu-ray/telemedicine, GM/file sharing, Spiderman/responsibility)
4. **Formal citations** (minimal, usually single factual claims)

## Transition Vocabulary

### Signature Phrases (appear in 5+ documents across both eras)
| Phrase | Function |
|--------|----------|
| **"With that being said"** | Pivot to counterpoint or qualification — #1 verbal fingerprint |
| **"However"** | Dominant contrast transition |
| **"For example" / "Take for example"** | Example introduction — extremely frequent |
| **"On the other hand"** | Explicit contrast |
| **"This in turn" / "which in turn"** | Cause-effect chaining |
| **"In essence"** | Reframing/simplifying |
| **"I personally"** | Opinion intensifier |
| **"ranging from... to"** | Scope listing construction |
| **"just to name a few"** | Closes enumerations |
| **"Unfortunately"** | Sentence-opening negative pivot |

### Absent Transitions (things Matthew does NOT say)
"Furthermore," "moreover," "consequently," "thus," "hence," "indeed," "notwithstanding." His transitions are conversational, not formal-academic.

## Vocabulary Profile

### Register: Mid-Level Conversational-Professional
Plain language with occasional domain-specific terms. Never reaches for SAT words or academic jargon. Writes closer to how he'd explain something verbally.

### Characteristic Words (recurring)
- **"a plethora of"** — favorite intensifier (3+ docs)
- **"repercussions"** — preferred over "consequences"
- **"detrimental"** — preferred over "harmful"
- **"deploy" / "deployment"** — preferred over "use" / "implement" (military origin)
- **"daunting"** — used for intimidating complexity
- **"arduous"** — used for difficult tasks
- **"facets"** — used for "aspects"
- **"foster" / "fostered"** — used for nurturing growth
- **"staggering"** — for emphasis on scale
- **"remedial"** — for mundane/basic tasks
- **"of recent"** — distinctive usage where standard English says "recently"

### Colloquialisms That Break Through
Matthew's conversational voice punctures the academic register regularly:
- "my credit is already bad enough"
- "didn't fare so well"
- "the boring and mundane"
- "stabbing its employees in the back"
- "a sinking ship" (used in multiple docs)
- "That is a rediculous accusation."

### Idioms and Cliches (favors familiar, everyman idioms)
- "with great power comes great responsibility" (used across 4 versions of one paper)
- "the road to Hell is paved with good intentions"
- "a well oiled working machine"
- "light at the end of a long tunnel"
- "locomotive bearing down the track"
- "reared its ugly head"

## Voice and Stance

### Hedging vs. Assertion
**Default: Assertive.** Hedges only on personal opinions, never on factual claims.

**Assertive markers:**
- "I firmly believe..."
- "I definitely think..."
- "I agree with this stance."
- "It is no secret that..."

**Hedging markers (narrow vocabulary):**
- "I believe" / "I personally feel" (opinion signals, not true hedging)
- "could" (hypotheticals)
- "in my opinion"
- Rarely uses "may," "might," "it seems," or "perhaps"

### Active vs. Passive Voice
**Overwhelmingly active.** Passive appears only in procedural/instructional contexts or when the actor is unknown.

### Personal Experience Integration
This is a defining feature. Matthew does not treat personal experience as anecdotal — he treats it as primary evidence and integrates it naturally:

> "Having been in the military and being deployed to Iraq from 2003 to 2004 I personally witnessed many of the complications..."

> "While I worked for Gamestop we enforced the ESRB game rating system."

> "I started using the application shortly after 2005 when hurricane Katrina destroyed the MS Gulf Coast area, my hometown."

> "I have often heard individuals, even including myself stating that it would be of no gain to steal identity, my credit is already bad enough."

The personal references are brief, self-deprecating, and used to build credibility or relatability — never for self-aggrandizement.

---

## Opening and Closing Patterns

### Openings: Broad Context → Narrow Focus
Never uses dramatic hooks, rhetorical questions, or anecdotal leads. Opens with a wide-lens contextual statement and narrows:

> "Social networking is a trend that has taken root in America, and for many it has become part of everyday life."

> "Some of the most famous robots have been idols of science fiction."

> "Crime is an unescapable part of human history."

### Closings: Moral/Responsibility Summation
Consistent closing pattern: **restate the stakes, invoke personal responsibility, end with a general truth.**

> "Ultimately, the responsibility falls to each of us to keep informed, to keep others informed and to stay safe."

> "There also comes a time when you need to realize your job as a parent and teach your children right from wrong."

> "The best way to make a decision is to make an informed decision."

## Tone

- **Earnest and genuine.** No irony, sarcasm, or cleverness-for-its-own-sake.
- **Explanatory.** Defines terms and provides background even when the audience likely knows them. "Make sure everyone is on the same page" tendency.
- **Measured but opinionated.** Presents information before stating his view, but does state it clearly.
- **Pragmatic over idealistic.** Ethical analysis grounded in practical reality (jobs, families, student loans), not abstract philosophy.
- **Pop-culture comfortable.** References to Elon Musk, science fiction, video games, Spiderman are natural — not forced.
- **Not sarcastic in writing.** Despite verbal humor, written work is straightforward.

## Voice Maturation (NAU 2010-2013 → Purdue 2017-2019)

### What Stayed the Same (Core Voice)
- "With that being said" pivot
- Personal experience as primary evidence
- Inductive argument structure
- Two-sided framing before committing
- Active voice dominance
- Conversational-professional register
- Broad-context openings, moral-summation closings

### What Evolved
| Dimension | NAU (Early) | Purdue (Mature) |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Sentence control | More run-ons, comma splices | Slightly cleaner clause management |
| Evidence quality | Heavy personal anecdote | Better balance of personal + current events |
| Vocabulary | More cliches and idioms | More domain-specific terms, fewer cliches |
| Hedging | Raw assertiveness ("That is rediculous") | More calibrated ("I believe", "I personally feel") |
| Argument depth | Surface-level two-sided | Deeper engagement with counterpoints |
| Pop culture references | Video games, movies | Tech industry, current events (Musk, Cambridge Analytica) |
| Closing sophistication | Simple moral statements | Layered pragmatic-ethical closings |

## Anti-Patterns (Things That Are NOT Matthew's Voice)

If writing sounds like this, it's not him:
- Formal academic register ("Furthermore," "Moreover," "Notwithstanding")
- Deductive logic (thesis → proof → QED)
- Citation-dense paragraphs
- Abstract philosophical frameworks without concrete examples
- Ironic or sarcastic tone
- Passive voice-heavy construction
- Original literary metaphors (he uses familiar idioms instead)
- Hedging-heavy language ("may," "might," "perhaps," "it seems")
- Opening with rhetorical questions or dramatic hooks

---

## Usage Guide

When generating content in Matthew's voice:
1. **Lead with a concrete example**, then derive the principle
2. **Use "With that being said"** when pivoting to a counterpoint
3. **Chain examples** with "For example" / "Take for example" / "Another example"
4. **Keep vocabulary mid-level** — professional but not academic
5. **Include brief personal references** that build credibility, not ego
6. **End with a practical, grounded takeaway** — not a lofty ideal
7. **Use active voice** almost exclusively
8. **Let conversational phrases break through** the professional register occasionally
9. **Present both sides** before committing to a position
10. **Avoid** formal academic transitions, passive voice, and abstract reasoning without examples
